## What Pinterest actually is

Pinterest is a visual search engine, not a social media platform.
This distinction changes everything about how to use it.

On Instagram, content dies in 48 hours. On Pinterest, a well-optimised
pin drives traffic for 6-18 months after it is published. The compound
effect of this is significant: 100 good pins published over 3 months
continue working for a year and a half. This is why Pinterest is the
right channel for a digital product business like Switzertemplates -
it builds passive, compounding traffic that does not require daily
content creation.

Pinterest users are planners, not scrollers. They come to Pinterest
with a specific intent: to find something, save it, and act on it
later. This is a fundamentally different mindset from Instagram or
TikTok. It means pins do not need to be entertaining - they need to
be useful, clear, and easy to save for future reference.

The primary metric that matters for Switzertemplates is outbound
clicks - the number of people who click through to the website or
Etsy shop. Saves and impressions are secondary. A pin with 10,000
impressions and 5 outbound clicks is less valuable than a pin with
800 impressions and 40 outbound clicks.

## How Pinterest search works

Pinterest ranks pins based on a combination of:

1. Keyword relevance - does the pin title and description contain the
   exact keyword the user searched for?
2. Pin quality - does the pin get saves, clicks, and close-ups after
   being shown in search results?
3. Domain quality - does the destination URL have a history of
   Pinterest traffic converting well?
4. Freshness - newer pins get a temporary boost in the first 2-4 weeks
5. Visual relevance - Pinterest's image recognition reads pin visuals
   and matches them to search intent

The most important of these for a new pin is keyword relevance. A pin
that does not contain the exact search keyword in the title will not
rank for that keyword, regardless of how good the image is.

The second most important is click-through rate. Pinterest interprets
a click as a signal that the pin matched search intent. This means
the pin headline must create enough curiosity or clarity that a
searcher clicks, not just saves.

## Keyword strategy for Switzertemplates

### The keyword hierarchy

Not all keywords are equal. There are three tiers:

**Tier 1 - High volume product keywords (target aggressively)**
These are keywords where someone is actively looking for what
Switzertemplates sells. Volume 5,000+, commercial intent, rising or
stable trend.
Examples: instagram templates, canva templates, wix website templates,
branding kit, coach website

**Tier 2 - Audience keywords (use only as compound terms)**
Audience keywords are never valid standalone. They must always be
combined with a product or solution modifier before use.
Examples: "life coaches" → "branding for life coaches",
"Instagram templates for life coaches".
Re-check compound volume via Keywords Everywhere API. Discard if under 300.

**Tier 3 - Problem-aware keywords (target with educational pins
that lead to product pins)**
These are keywords where someone is aware they have a branding or
website problem and is looking for guidance. Volume 300+.
Examples: branding for small businesses, branding identity, how to
build a personal brand, social media templates

### Keyword intent signals to eliminate

Always eliminate keywords where the searcher intent does not lead
toward buying templates or websites:

- "free" in the keyword - non-buyer
- "jobs" in the keyword - job seeker, not business owner
- "app" suffix - looking for software, not templates
- "what is" / "what does" prefix - dictionary search, no commercial
  intent
- "how much do X make" - income research, not buying
- "login" suffix - existing platform user navigating, not buying
- Year suffix (2018, 2019, 2020) - stale query, wrong version intent
- Platform navigation (canva uk, canva desktop) - going to Canva
  directly, not buying from us
- Comparison shopping (best website builder) - decision stage, not
  buying stage for templates

### Keyword placement rules (non-negotiable)

Pinterest's algorithm reads pin titles and descriptions as search
index fields. The rules are:

1. The primary keyword must appear in the first 4 words of the title
2. The primary keyword must appear in the first sentence of the
   description
3. The keyword must appear naturally - forced or awkward placement
   hurts click-through rate
4. Secondary keywords can be woven into the rest of the description
   naturally
5. Never keyword-stuff - Pinterest penalises descriptions that read
   like a tag list

## What makes a Pinterest pin perform

### Title principles

The title is the most important copy element on a pin. Pinterest
displays it below the image in search results. It determines whether
someone clicks or keeps scrolling.

Principles:
- Lead with the keyword (first 4 words)
- Make a specific promise or observation, not a generic label
- Keep it under 100 characters
- Sentence case only - capital letter at the start of the title, not every word
- Write for the person who will see it 6 months from now, not today
- The best Pinterest titles make the searcher feel like the pin was
  made specifically for them

Primary title style - Jane's action-led, direct format (use this most):
These come from Jane's actual top pins. Lead with action or outcome.
- "Start your business faster with [keyword]"
- "Save time using [keyword] for your [context]"
- "Get more clients using [keyword]"
- "Look professional online with [keyword]"
- "[Keyword] - look professional and attract more clients"

Examples:
"Start your coaching business with a premade Wix website"
"Save time using business coach templates for your content"
"Get more clients using a professional Wix website for coaches"
"Branding kit for coaches - look professional and attract more clients"

Secondary title style - keyword-that-outcome (use occasionally to test variety):
- "[Keyword] that [specific outcome]"

Examples:
"Coach websites that convert - built on Wix, no coding needed"
"Instagram templates that make your feed look intentional"
"Branding kit that makes your business look established from day one"

Other approved structures (use sparingly):
- "A [keyword] ready to [specific action] in [time frame]"
  Example: "A branding kit ready to use across every platform today"
- "For [audience]: [keyword] that [solves their problem]"
  Example: "For coaches: a Wix website that wins clients before they reach out"

Title structures to avoid:
- Generic labels: "Branding kit for small business" - no promise, no pull
- Hypey claims: "The ultimate branding kit" - not credible
- Question as a hook: "Need a branding kit?" - weak on Pinterest
- Keyword-only: "Instagram templates Canva" - reads like a tag, not a title
- Leading with a CTA or command: "Get your templates now" - too pushy for Pinterest

### Description principles

Pinterest descriptions serve two purposes: they are indexed by
Pinterest's search algorithm, and they are read by people who click
on a pin to see more before deciding whether to click through.

Principles:
- First sentence contains the primary keyword naturally
- Two to three sentences maximum for the core message
- Lead with the problem or outcome, not the product features
- Include a secondary keyword naturally in the second sentence
- End with an approved CTA or close on the value statement (no CTA)
- Total length: 150-300 characters is optimal for Pinterest indexing
- Explain the mechanism, not just the outcome. Don't just say "look
  professional" — say how the product makes that happen and why it matters.
  Wrong: "Branding kit that makes your business look professional."
  Right: "Branding kit with 1,000+ templates across social media, email,
  and print - so every platform looks consistent and your business feels
  established before a client even reaches out."
- Use the "so you can" construction to connect a feature to a real benefit.
- Close simply. End on the value or the CTA. No dramatic final lines,
  no "you've got this", no grand closers. Just stop when the message is done.

Structure:
Sentence 1: Keyword + problem or outcome (searchable)
Sentence 2: What the product does / what the pin teaches — the mechanism
Sentence 3: CTA from the approved list, or end on the value if no CTA

Description opening rotation — non-negotiable across a topic batch:
The opening of each description must vary across the 5 variations for
the same keyword. No two consecutive variations can open the same way.
Rotate between these four modes:

1. Verb-led (action first):
   "Get your [keyword] live this week without starting from scratch..."
   "Save time building your [keyword] by starting from a premade design..."

2. Problem-led (name the frustration):
   "[Keyword] that feels generic is costing you clients before they
   even read your bio..."
   "Starting from scratch on your [keyword] takes weeks you don't have..."

3. Transformation-led (before and after):
   "[Keyword] that takes you from no online presence to fully live
   in days - not months..."

4. Adjective-led (describe the product quality first):
   "A professional [keyword] that covers every page your business
   needs - services, about, contact, and booking..."
   "A premade [keyword] designed for coaches who want to look
   established from day one..."

Every description ends with 2-3 relevant hashtags — no spaces, no punctuation
between them, placed after the final sentence or CTA on the same line or
on the line below. Hashtags must be topically relevant to the keyword and product.

Product hashtag examples by category:
- Wix website pins: #wixwebsitetemplate #premadewebsite #coachwebsite #wixtemplate
- Branding kit pins: #smallbusinessbranding #brandidentitytips #premadebranding #brandingkit
- Instagram templates: #instagramtemplates #canvatemplates #socialmediatemplates
- General audience: #smallbusinesstips #onlinebusiness #servicebusiness

Example for "instagram templates":
"Instagram templates built for coaches and service providers who want
a feed that looks professional without spending hours on design. 1,000+
Canva templates across posts, stories, and carousels - ready to customise
and post so your content looks consistent every week. Click to browse!
#instagramtemplates #canvatemplates #smallbusinesstips"

### Pin headline vs SEO title — two different fields

Every pin has two text fields that serve different purposes. Never write
the same text for both.

**pin_headline** (text displayed ON the pin image — stops the scroll):
- A frustration, realisation, or ambition — not a product pitch
- 5-15 words, all lowercase
- Should feel like a thought, not an ad
- Examples:
  "your brand tells clients if you're worth hiring before you say a word"
  "a scattered brand is costing you clients before they even reach out"
  "your website is working for you 24/7 - or it isn't"
  "the gap between looking established and looking DIY is smaller than you think"

**seo_title** (the Pinterest metadata title — what ranks in search):
- Keyword in the first 4 words
- Action-led or benefit-led (use the primary title style above)
- 50-100 characters, sentence case
- This is what the algorithm indexes and what searchers read in results

### Pin copy formatting rules (non-negotiable)

These apply to every title, description, and headline written for Switzertemplates.

- Headlines (pin_headline) always start with a capital letter.
  Right: "Your website is working for you 24/7 - or it isn't"
  Wrong: "your website is working for you 24/7 - or it isn't"
- Use a regular dash ( - ) not an em dash ( — )
- Dashes in seo_title only when separating two genuinely distinct clauses.
  Never use a dash where "to" flows naturally.
  Right: "Wix website template to look professional and attract more clients"
  Wrong: "Wix website template - look professional and attract more clients"
  Right with dash: "Coach websites that convert - built on Wix, no coding needed"
  (two distinct clauses — the dash is separating what it is from how it works)
- Sentence case only. Capital letter at the start, not every word.
  Right: "Premade Wix website templates for coaches"
  Wrong: "Premade Wix Website Templates For Coaches"
- No rhetorical question-then-answer. Don't ask a question then immediately answer it.
  Wrong: "Want more clients? Here's how to get them."
- No grand inspiring statements. Keep it grounded and specific.
  Wrong: "Transform your business and step into your power."
- Never repeat the title word-for-word in the description. Restate the value differently.
- Use the "so you can" construction to connect a feature to a real benefit.
  Example: "Business coach templates give you a repeatable format for your posts,
  so you can show up consistently without overthinking every piece of content."

### Design brief principles

The image is what stops the scroll. On Pinterest, text-overlay pins
perform significantly better than pure lifestyle photography for
digital product businesses. The image needs to communicate the product
or the idea before the person reads a single word.

What works for Switzertemplates specifically:
- Device mockups (laptop, phone, tablet) showing the actual product
  in use outperform styled flat lays for product pins
- For educational pins, a bold single-line text overlay on a clean
  background outperforms cluttered informational graphics
- Warm editorial tones (cream, terracotta, sage, dusty pink, warm
  white) match the audience aesthetic and the brand
- Text overlays should be large enough to read in a thumbnail
- The pin ratio is 2:3 (1000x1500px) - content in the top two thirds,
  branding in the bottom third
- Category label top-left, watermark bottom-right (switzertemplates.com)

Design brief specificity rules:
- Name the device: "laptop", "phone", "tablet" not "device"
- Name the surface: "warm linen", "marble", "oak desk" not "surface"
- Name the tone: "cream and terracotta" not "warm tones"
- Name the light: "soft natural light from the left" not "good lighting"
- Name the text position: "text overlay space in the upper-left third"
- A vague brief produces a generic image. Every brief must be
  specific enough that two different designers would produce similar results.

## CTA rules

CTAs must rotate across pins so no two consecutive pins use the same one.
Using no CTA at all is a valid option — rotate it in occasionally so
descriptions don't feel formulaic.

Approved CTAs (rotate through these for both product and educational pins):
- "Click to browse!"
- "Grab it in my Etsy store!"
- "Have a browse!"
- "Check it out -->"
- "Click to have a look -->"
- (no CTA — close on the value statement, then hashtags)

CTA formatting rules (non-negotiable):
- Every CTA starts with a capital letter
- Every CTA ends with either ! or --> — never a full stop
- Never end a description with a plain sentence that sounds like a CTA but has no punctuation

Never use:
- "Buy now" - too aggressive
- "Click here" - generic, no value signal
- "Discover" - banned word in brand voice
- "Shop now" - too pushy
- "Download now" - wrong product type
- "Get yours today" - too salesy
- "Full guide on the blog" or any variation — the URL already signals where they're going
- Any phrasing that narrates the link destination: "Read the full breakdown on the blog",
  "Full breakdown on the blog", "Get the full guide on the blog" — all banned
  The description should close on the value, not on a redundant direction-pointer.

## Learning from Pinterest account analytics

The agent must read pin performance data from the Pinterest API at
the start of every run and use it to inform current decisions.

### What to extract from analytics

For each of the top 50 pins by impressions, record:
- outbound_click_rate (clicks to website / impressions) - primary signal
- save_rate (saves / impressions) - secondary signal
- close_up_rate (close-ups / impressions) - intent signal
- title structure - what headline pattern was used
- keyword used - what the pin was targeting
- pin type - product or educational

### How to use the data

**Title patterns:**
Identify which title structures in the top 10 pins by outbound click
rate share common patterns. If "A [product] ready to [action]"
appears 3 times in the top 10, that structure should be used in
at least 30% of new pin titles.

**Keyword performance:**
If a keyword has pins with outbound click rate above 0.5% it is
performing well. Prioritise creating more pin variations for it.
If a keyword has pins with outbound click rate below 0.1% despite
good impressions, the pin copy or product match needs rethinking.

**Product performance:**
If Wix website pins consistently outperform branding kit pins on
outbound clicks, weight the topic selection toward Wix website
keywords in the current batch.

**What to ignore:**
High save rate with low outbound click rate means the pin is
inspiring but not converting. This is common for educational pins.
Do not optimise for saves at the expense of clicks. Saves build
brand awareness but they do not directly drive revenue.

### Signal threshold for changing strategy

Only change strategy if a pattern appears in 3 or more pins.
Single-pin performance is not statistically meaningful. Look for
patterns across at least 5 pins before drawing conclusions.

## Performance feedback loop

After every --generate run, save data/pinterest-agent/batch-log-[YYYY-MM-DD].json
with one entry per pin variation: pin title, keyword, product mapped,
destination URL, batch date.

At the start of the next --generate run, pull outbound click rate from
the Pinterest API for all pins recorded in the most recent batch log.

Score each keyword from the previous batch:

| Outbound click rate | Condition | Action |
|---------------------|-----------|--------|
| Above 0.5% | Any impressions | Increase volume: generate more variations for this keyword in the next batch |
| 0.1% – 0.5% | Any impressions | Maintain: keep current approach |
| Below 0.1% | Good impressions (500+) | Flag title structure for review — the keyword may be right but the headline is not converting |
| Below 0.1% | Low impressions (under 500) | Insufficient data — carry forward unchanged |

Never act on fewer than 3 pins per keyword. Single-pin data is not
statistically meaningful. Only change strategy once 3 or more pins
for the same keyword have accumulated performance data.

## Seasonal and timing awareness

Pinterest search volume shifts seasonally. The agent should adjust
keyword priority based on time of year.

Q1 (Jan-Mar): New year, new business energy. Keywords around starting
a business, rebranding, and launching perform above baseline.
Peak keywords: "how to start an online business", "personal branding",
"business coach website", "new business branding"

Q2 (Apr-Jun): Spring momentum. Service businesses pick up, coaches
launch new programs. Template buying increases.
Peak keywords: "instagram templates", "canva templates", "branding kit",
"coach website"

Q3 (Jul-Sep): Mid-year audit season. Business owners reassess their
online presence. Good time for educational pins about what is not
working.
Peak keywords: "branding examples", "social media templates",
"wix website templates", "virtual assistant branding"

Q4 (Oct-Dec): End of year planning, budget spending, pre-launch for
January. Digital product gifting.
Peak keywords: "business bundle", "branding kit", "wix website",
"canva instagram templates"

## Pin variation strategy per topic

Each topic gets 5 pin variations. The split is determined by PM score:

For PM=3 (direct product match — e.g. "instagram templates", "branding kit"):
- 4 product pins + 1 educational pin
- Each product pin must use a different visual angle and different
  headline structure
- The educational pin should target a related long-tail keyword

For PM=2 (product-adjacent — e.g. "coach website", "canva template"):
- 3 product pins + 2 educational pins

For PM=1 (audience keyword with product modifier — e.g. "branding for coaches"):
- 2 product pins + 3 educational pins
- Educational pins lead with the problem, product pins lead with
  the solution

For PM=0 (awareness / minimal product signal):
- 1 product pin + 4 educational pins

Across the full 5 variations per topic:
- No two variations can use the same CTA
- No two variations can use the same headline structure
- No two variations can use the same visual approach
- Each variation must feel like a completely different pin even
  though it targets the same keyword

10. Is the pin_headline different from the seo_title — a hook or observation, not a product title?

12. Does the description repeat the title word-for-word? Rewrite if so.

16. Are there any banned words present? (See the full list in "What this agent must never do")
17. Is "The Switzertemplates X" replaced with "Our X" everywhere?
18. Does the pin make sense for someone who will see it in 6 months, not just today?

If any check fails, rewrite before outputting.

## Competitor Intelligence

This section contains direct instructions based on competitor analysis of 75 pins across 4 accounts in the digital product and design space. Apply these rules every time you generate pin copy or recommend visual direction.

---

### Title Structure Rules

**Use these structures:**
- **Statement titles** are your default format. Over half of all competitor pins use declarative, keyword-rich statements that describe exactly what the product is or does. Lead with clarity, not cleverness.
- **Keyword-that-outcome** is your second choice. Structure titles as "[keyword] that [specific benefit]" — for example, "Shopify theme that helps authors sell more books." This format wins on both search discoverability and persuasion. Use it when you have a clear, provable benefit to attach.
- **Audience-colon** titles should be used sparingly but deliberately when targeting a specific niche persona. Format: "For [Audience]: [What It Does]." This signals relevance immediately and filters for high-intent users.

**Avoid this structure:**
- Never lead with a CTA or imperative command (e.g., "Get your templates now" or "Download this theme"). Only 1 in 75 competitor pins uses this format — this audience does not respond to pushy, directive titles on Pinterest. Save CTAs for the pin description, not the title.
