# brand-voice.md — Switzertemplates

This is the single source of truth for tone, style, and writing rules.
Every agent reads this before writing anything for Switzertemplates.

---

## The voice in one sentence

Clear, practical, and human. Like a knowledgeable friend who has built
a real business and is telling you exactly what worked - without the fluff.

---

## Style blend

The writing should feel like a combination of three influences:

**Jane's own voice (primary):**
Warm but direct. Practical over inspirational. Grounded in real examples.
Writes the way she talks - no performance, no fluff, no unnecessary words.
Her emails feel like they come from a real person, not a brand.

**Matt Gray (structure and directness):**
Short sentences. One idea per line. Numbered or broken-up points that
are easy to scan. Gets to the value fast. Never buries the lead.
Builds trust by being specific and useful before asking for anything.

**Sunshine Seeker (neutral, human, simple):**
Writes in first person. Gets to the point quickly. Short posts with
personal perspective woven in. Doesn't over-explain. Sounds like a
real person sharing what they know, not a content machine.

**A Passion and A Passport (light humour, warmth):**
Uses a little wit to make the writing feel more alive and less corporate.
Adds personality through small asides, honest admissions, and a slightly
cheeky tone when it fits. This element is used sparingly - never in
selling texts, product descriptions, or ads. Only in educational content,
emails, and blog posts where it feels natural and appropriate.

## Core writing rules

These apply to every piece of content, every channel, every time.

**Voice:**
- Use plain language and short, direct sentences
- Use active voice
- Speak directly to the reader using "you" and "your"
- Sound human, not robotic or formal
- One idea per sentence where possible
- Every sentence earns its place - no padding

**Structure:**
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences for social and email)
- Start with the most important or surprising point
- Move quickly to practical advice
- Use bullet points for lists or steps - not for everything
- Blog posts can go longer but never walls of text

**Formatting:**
- Do not use em dashes ( — ) - use a regular dash ( - ) instead
- For parenthetical thoughts, use brackets: (like this) not — like this —
- No rhetorical question-then-answer tricks
- No grand "inspiring" statements that sound written
- Starts somewhere honest and builds naturally to a simple truth
- Capital letter at the start of each sentence only
- Do not capitalise every word in a sentence or heading

**Content:**
- Focus on actionable guidance - what to do, why it matters, how to start
- Use specific examples when helpful
- Avoid theory without a takeaway
- Connect design to a business outcome, always
- Make benefits concrete: look professional, attract clients, save time,
  build trust, drive traffic, make sales

## Banned words and phrases

Never use these under any circumstances:

certainly, delve, embark, enlightening, esteemed, shed light, craft,
imagine, realm, game-changer, illuminate, unlock, discover, pivotal,
skyrocket, abyss, folks, furthermore, harness, groundbreaking,
cutting-edge, remarkable, glimpse, navigating, landscape, stark,
moreover, boost

Also avoid:
- "clarity", "alignment", "journey", "strategy" without a concrete
  benefit immediately following
- "You've got this!" and similar hollow closers
- "Level up" and similar overused business phrases
- "Exciting news!" as an opener
- "Have you ever wondered..." as a hook
- Overly salesy, hyped, or pushy language

## What great writing looks like - with examples

**From Jane's actual emails:**

"Most people hear the word "funnel" and immediately picture complicated
software, confusing diagrams, and a tech setup that takes three weeks
to figure out. It doesn't have to be any of that."

Why it works: opens with a real objection, immediately reframes it,
no fluff, no build-up.

"Social media is rented land. The algorithm changes, reach drops,
platforms come and go. Your website is the one place online you actually
own - and it works for you 24/7."

Why it works: punchy contrast, specific benefit, zero padding.

"I spent two years growing my Etsy shop before I ever thought seriously
about email. Then one month, reach dropped significantly and I had no
backup."

Why it works: personal, specific, tells a story without over-explaining.

**From Matt Gray's style:**
Starts with a clear premise. Breaks it into scannable points.
Each point is one idea, fully delivered. No filler between them.
Closes with one action or one clear takeaway.

**From Sunshine Seeker:**
Writes like she is talking to a friend. Gets into the useful stuff fast.
Short sentences. First-person perspective that feels real, not performed.

**From A Passion and A Passport (use sparingly):**
Wit and warmth without being unprofessional. Small asides, honest
admissions, a touch of humour that makes the reader feel like they are
reading something written by an actual human. Only use this energy in
blog posts and educational emails - never in product listings or ads.

## Channel-specific rules

---

### Pinterest

**Goal:** drive clicks from buyers, not browsers.

**Title rules:**
- 50-100 characters
- Include the primary keyword naturally in the first half
- Benefit-led or problem-led
- No clickbait, no unnecessary question marks

**Strong title formats (from Jane's actual pins):**
- "Start your business faster with this business marketing bundle"
- "Save time using Business coach templates for your content"
- "Ready to look more professional? Start with [keyword]"
- "Get more clients using [keyword]"

**Description rules:**
- 150-300 characters
- Lead with the main benefit or pain point
- Include 2-3 natural keywords
- End with a soft CTA: "Click to browse" or "Shop at switzertemplates.com"
- Maximum 2-3 hashtags if used at all
- Never repeat the title word-for-word in the description

**Good description example (from Jane's actual pins):**
"This business marketing bundle gives you a complete setup with a
branding kit, a Wix website, and a Canva landing page - so your business
looks consistent across every platform and attracts clients faster."

**What to avoid:**
- "Amazing", "stunning", "gorgeous" - show the benefit, don't exclaim it
- Keyword stuffing that reads unnaturally
- Generic descriptions that could apply to any template business

---

### Email

**Goal:** convert warm past buyers into repeat customers.
This list already trusts the brand. The job is to remind them what else
exists and why they need it now.

**Subject line rules:**
- Under 50 characters
- Curiosity-driven, benefit-led, or problem-framing
- Never vague
- Never misleading

**Good subject line examples:**
- "Your website is losing you clients"
- "The template 800+ coaches use"
- "What I'd do if I had to start again"
- "The simplest funnel for an online business"

**Preview text rules:**
- Adds to the subject line - never repeats it
- Completes the thought or adds a second hook
- Under 90 characters

**Body copy rules:**
- Opens with a hook - a specific observation, relatable frustration,
  or a short personal story
- Never opens with "Hi lovely, I hope you're well" type filler
- Short paragraphs, one idea per paragraph
- Every email gives something before it asks for anything
- One clear CTA per email
- CTA copy is action-led and specific: "Get the 3-in-1 bundle"
  not "Click here" or "Shop now"
- Length: 150-350 words for promotional. Story-driven emails up to 500.
- Sign off: "Xo Jane" - keep it personal and consistent

**Good email opener example (from Jane's writing):**
"Most people hear the word "funnel" and immediately picture complicated
software, confusing diagrams, and a tech setup that takes three weeks
to figure out. It doesn't have to be any of that."

**Sequence logic:**
- New Instagram template buyer: welcome + tips for use - then introduce
  Wix websites ("your templates deserve a real home") - then introduce
  3-in-1 bundle for those who haven't bought a website or branding kit
- New website buyer: welcome + setup tips - then introduce branding kit
  ("make everything match") - then share a customer result
- Lapsed buyer (90+ days): re-engagement with new arrival or relevant offer

---

### Instagram

**Goal:** build awareness, grow audience, funnel to email list or website.

**Caption rules:**
- Opens with a hook - one line that stops the scroll
- Flows naturally like a thought, not a structured list
- Educates, resonates, or challenges a belief
- Ends with a soft CTA or open question
- Hashtags go in first comment or after a line break at the end

**Good hook examples (from Jane's actual carousels):**
- "There's a season in business that nobody warns you about..."
- "Getting traffic to your website means nothing if visitors leave
  without taking action."

**Weak hooks to avoid:**
- "Exciting news!"
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "Let's talk about branding today!"

**Carousel script rules:**
- Slide 1: hook - one punchy line, no topic announcement
- Slides 2-5: one idea per slide, short sentences
- Final slide: soft CTA - link in bio, save this, or DM
- Never more than 3-4 lines of text per slide
- Never announce "today I'm going to teach you" - just teach

**Story rules:**
- Hook slide first - a relatable problem or business frustration
- Middle slides: education, tips, or product showcase
- Final slide: clear CTA
- Text is short and scannable
- Never announce the lesson - lead with the value directly

### Blog (switzertemplates.com)

**Goal:** rank on Google for commercial-intent keywords, drive Pinterest
traffic, soft-sell relevant products throughout.

**Post structure:**
- Title: includes primary keyword, under 60 characters for SEO
- Intro (100-150 words): hooks with a relatable problem, previews what
  the post covers, establishes why it matters
- Body: practical and specific. Uses H2 subheadings. Each section
  answers one clear question.
- Tone: like a knowledgeable friend giving honest, experienced advice -
  not a corporate explainer or a how-to article written for robots
- Light humour is welcome when it fits naturally (A Passion and A Passport
  style) - never in product-selling sections or CTAs
- Product CTA: natural, not forced. Appears once mid-post and once at end.

**Good CTA example:**
"If you want a done-for-you version, the Switzertemplates 3-in-1 bundle
includes a full branding kit, a Wix website, and a Canva landing page -
ready to customise in minutes."

**Keyword targeting:**
Focus on commercial-intent searches. The reader should be one click
away from purchasing.

Strong keyword examples:
- "best Wix templates for life coaches"
- "branding kit for small business owners"
- "how to make your business look professional online"
- "premade website for coaches"
- "Canva branding templates for service providers"

**Length:** 800-1,500 words.

## Quick reference: do vs. don't

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Connect design to a business result | Praise aesthetics without a benefit |
| Use specific numbers and facts | Use vague superlatives |
| Write short, punchy sentences | Write long, clause-heavy sentences |
| Make CTAs specific and action-led | Use "click here" or "learn more" |
| Sound like a real person | Sound like a newsletter template |
| Use regular dashes ( - ) | Use em dashes ( — ) |
| Use brackets for asides (like this) | Use em dashes for asides |
| Let content educate naturally | Announce what you're about to teach |
| Start with the most useful point | Build up slowly to the main idea |
| Reference real proof (sales, reviews) | Make unsubstantiated claims |
