# content-style-examples.md — Switzertemplates

This file contains real examples of Switzertemplates content provided
by Jane as style and tone references. These are not confirmed top
performers - they are examples of how Jane writes and what her content
should sound like.

For actual performance data (what converts, what gets saves, what drives

## Email examples

### Email: "The simplest funnel for an online business"

**What works about this email:**
Opens by immediately dismantling a common misconception. Gets into the
practical content fast. Uses bullet-pointed sections but each section
reads like a real person talking, not a marketing template. Closes with
a soft sell that feels like a natural next step, not a pitch.

**Opening (study this pattern):**
"Most people hear the word "funnel" and immediately picture complicated
software, confusing diagrams, and a tech setup that takes three weeks
to figure out. It doesn't have to be any of that."

**Key section - how Jane talks about her own content:**
"For me, that means branding tips for coaches and business owners,
before-and-after Instagram makeovers using my templates, or quick
business hacks people can apply to their own niche today."

Note: personal but not self-centred. The example is about her but
the takeaway is always for the reader.

**Key section - how Jane handles the email list:**
"When someone opens your email, they've chosen to be there. That's a
completely different level of attention."

"You don't need to email every day. Once a week or even twice a month,
consistently, is enough to stay in someone's mind. The key word is
consistently."

"One simple rule: every email should give something before it asks
for anything."

**How it closes:**
"For you, it might look completely different. But the principle is the
same: make it easy to understand, easy to buy, and directly connected
to the problem your content has been talking about all along."

**Sign off:** "If you have any questions about my products or services,
feel free to reply to this email - I'll be happy to help you! Xo Jane."

### Email: "What I'd do if I had to start my business again"

**What works about this email:**
Uses personal backstory to deliver practical business advice. Each point
is a lesson drawn from real experience. The product mentions feel earned,
not inserted. The language is grounded and specific - not motivational.

**Opening section - niching down:**
"My first year, I was designing everything. Wedding invitations, recipe
cards, birthday party packs, café menus. If someone needed it, I made
it. But at the end of that year, I had a scattered portfolio and no
clear direction."

"The shift came when I stopped designing for everyone and focused
entirely on businesses - specifically small business owners and
service-based businesses wanting to grow online. Suddenly the right
people started finding me, and my work finally made sense."

**Takeaway format (study this):**
"A takeaway for you: If you're saying yes to everything right now, try
narrowing it down. The more specific you are about who you serve, the
easier it is for them to find you - and trust you immediately."

**Section - brand identity:**
"I see this constantly: businesses operating for years with a Canva
logo, three different fonts across their platforms, and colours that
don't quite match anywhere. It looks unintentional - and unintentional
poor-looking branding erodes trust before a customer even reaches out."

"You don't need a huge budget. You need a clear logo, a small set of
colours, two-three fonts, and a consistent style - applied in a similar
way across all your platforms."

**Section - website:**
"Social media is rented land. The algorithm changes, reach drops,
platforms come and go. Your website is the one place online you actually
own - and it works for you 24/7."

"You don't need something elaborate. A clear homepage, a services or
product page, and a way to get in touch. That's enough to build real
credibility."

**Section - email:**
"I spent two years growing my Etsy shop before I ever thought seriously
about email. Then one month, reach dropped significantly and I had no
backup. If I'd had an email list, it would have helped me to still
reach my customers and keep making sales."

"Your list is the only audience you truly own. Even 200 engaged
subscribers will drive more results than thousands of passive viewers."

### Pins for keyword: "Business marketing bundle"

These perform well because they lead with a clear action or outcome,
include the keyword naturally, and the descriptions spell out exactly
what is included without being generic.

**Pin 1:**
Title: Start your business faster with this business marketing bundle
Description: Our business marketing bundle includes a full branding kit,
a Wix website for coaches, and a Canva landing page - so you can set up
your business properly without building everything from scratch.
Click to have a browse! ->>

**Pin 2:**
Title: Want to launch properly? Use this business marketing bundle
Description: With our 3-in-1 business marketing bundle, you get a full
branding kit, a ready-to-use Wix website, and a Canva landing page!
Everything to launch your business in a couple of days and start scaling.

**Pin 3:**
Title: Save time using this all-in-one business marketing bundle
Description: Instead of creating everything separately, this business
marketing bundle gives you a branding kit, a Wix website for coaches,
and a Canva landing page in one place, so you can focus on growing
your business.

**What these have in common:**
- Keyword appears naturally in the first half of the title
- Description immediately explains what is included
- Benefit is stated clearly (launch faster, save time, look professional)
- No hype words - no "stunning", "amazing", "incredible"
- The description reads like a helpful summary, not an ad

## Instagram carousel examples

---

### Carousel: "The season you can't skip as an entrepreneur"

**What works:**
Opens with mystery. Each slide builds on the last. Uses the slow season
(a real fear for business owners) and reframes it as an advantage. The
language is grounded and honest - not pep-talky.

**Slide structure:**
- Cover: "The season you can't skip as an entrepreneur (that nobody
  talks about)"
- Slide 1: "There's a season in business that nobody warns you about..."
- Slide 2: "But it's the most important one you'll go through"
- Slide 3: "The slow season. When every business hits a wall that feels
  like failure. But..."
- Slide 4: "The silence doesn't mean nothing is happening. Growth is
  mostly invisible before it's obvious. The work you're putting in is
  compounding underneath the surface, even when it doesn't look like
  it yet."
- Slide 5: "Boring consistency is your advantage. If you can be
  consistent when it's boring, you're already ahead of most people."
- Slide 6: "Use this time for building even harder. The quiet gives
  you something busy seasons don't - space."
- Slide 7: "The season that builds resilience. Resilience is built in
  seasons like this one, when you choose to keep going even though
  nothing is confirming that you should."
- Slide 8: "You're not behind. You're not failing. You're just in the
  season that most people quit. Keep going."

### Carousel: "How to make your website convert"

**What works:**
Practical and specific. Each slide covers one clear thing. The language
is direct without being harsh. Includes a personal product mention that
feels natural rather than forced.

**Slide structure:**
- Cover: "6 things you need for your website to convert"
- Slide 1: "Getting traffic to your website means nothing if visitors
  leave without taking action. Do these 6 things to turn browsers into
  actual buyers."
- Slide 2 (easy navigation): "If someone lands on your site and doesn't
  immediately know where to go or what to do - they leave. Keep it as
  simple as possible - one clear path, no visual noise, no decision
  fatigue."
- Slide 3 (clear CTA): "Every page should have one job - make a visitor
  take action. If your visitor has to figure out what to do next,
  you've already lost them."
- Slide 4 (mobile friendly): "Most people will find your website on
  their phone. If it's slow, cluttered, or hard to navigate on mobile,
  Google buries it and visitors bounce."

**Note on product mentions:**
Jane references her Wix websites naturally within the educational
content - not as a hard sell. Example: "All my Wix websites are
mobile-friendly, but always check the mobile version in the editor
before clicking publish." This is the correct approach. The product
is mentioned as part of the advice, not as the point of the slide.

## Patterns that appear across all top content

Study these before writing anything:

**1. Opens by naming a real situation or frustration**
Not an announcement. Not a question. A statement that makes the reader
think "that's exactly right."

**2. Moves fast**
The first two sentences earn the next two. There is no slow build-up.
No "in today's email I'll be covering..."

**3. Uses "you" constantly**
The content is always about the reader's business, the reader's
situation, the reader's outcome. Never about the brand performing
for an audience.

**4. Personal examples serve the reader's takeaway**
When Jane mentions her own experience, it is always in service of a
point the reader can use. It never lingers on her story for its own sake.

**5. Product mentions feel like recommendations, not pitches**
When a product appears, it's because it solves the problem being
discussed. It is introduced as a helpful option, not a hard sell.

**6. Closes simply**
No dramatic final lines. No "you've got this." A clear, grounded close
that either gives the reader something to do or something to think about.

**7. Uses dashes ( - ) not em dashes ( — )**
Consistent across all content. Short dash only.

**8. No banned words**
Review the banned word list in brand-voice.md before finalising
any piece of content.
